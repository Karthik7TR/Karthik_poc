Reason dictates that “a stay of creditors from collecting their claims against the debtor and his property from and after the filing of a petition under the Bankruptcy Act is indispensable to bankruptcy administration.” 1 Congress has explained this need for an automatic stay as follows:
The automatic stay is one of the fundamental debtor protections provided bankruptcy laws. It gives the debtor a breathing spell from his creditors. It stops all collection effort, all harassment, and all foreclosure actions. It permits the debtor to attempt a repayment or reorganization plan, or simply to be relieved of the financial pressure that drove him into bankruptcy.
The automatic stay also provides creditor protection. Without it, certain creditors would be able to pursue their own remedies against debtor's property. Those who acted first would obtain payment of claims in preference to and to the detriment of other creditors. Bankruptcy is designed to provide an orderly liquidation procedure under which all creditors are treated equally. A race of diligence by creditors for the debtor's assets prevents that. 2
Indeed, absent some means for staying the continued pursuit of nonbankruptcy remedies, effective access to relief under the Bankruptcy Code depends entirely on the unanimous support of creditors.
Recognizing both the unlikelihood of such uniform creditor acquiescence and the instinctively nonutilitarian nature of marketplace realities, the Bankruptcy Code section 362 provides for an automatic stay upon adverse creditor action. 3 By necessity, the “scope of [ section 362's provisions in this respect are] broad, but they do not apply automatically to non-debtor entities. 4 [Indeed,] all proceedings are stayed, including arbitration, license revocation, administrative, and judicial proceedings.” 5 Logically, this protection extends to nonbankruptcy litigation against the debtor, 6 as well as any related appeals 7 or processes. 8 Still further, as a consequence of section 362's mandate, the Bankruptcy Code also effects a stay upon acts of collection, lien enforcement, 9 and other judicial or nonjudicial actions which might constitute an attempt to enforce or collect upon a prepetition claim. 10 Also Also stayed are a wide range of actions which might interfere with property of the estate, the debtor, or the integrity of the bankruptcy proceedings. 11
Actions taken in violation of the Code's automatic stay provisions are void ab initio or voidable, and without legal effect. 12 Furthermore, it should be noted that parties willfully violating the automatic stay may find themselves subject to compensatory and punitive damages. 13 For a violation of the automatic stay to be “willful” within the meaning of section 362(k), the court must only find that the actor intended its actions and had notice of the existence of the automatic stay. 14 Once the willfulness of the violation has been established, an “individual” injured by the violation must be awarded reasonable and necessary attorneys fees and compensatory damages. 15 The court has discretion as to whether to award punitive damages, and most courts require a showing of egregious conduct before doing so. 16 Note that an action for damages for violating the automatic stay survives the dismissal of the bankruptcy case and the bankruptcy court will retain jurisdiction to hear and determine the matter. 17

Thus, broad as its terms may seem as an initial matter, the automatic stay has definite parameters of application, and its terms come subject to many limitations.
When applicable, the stay of section 362 continues until the case is closed or dismissed, a discharge is granted or denied, or the property in question is no longer part of the estate. 42 A party in interest may move the court for relief from the stay (terminating, annulling, modifying, or conditioning the stay) under the following grounds:
  • (1) for cause, 43 which may include the fact that the case was filed in bad faith 44 or the lack of adequate protection; 45
  • (2) because debtor does not have equity in the property in question, 46 and such property is not necessary for an effective reorganization; 47
  • (3) in a single asset real estate case, 48 the debtor has not either filed a confirmable plan or commenced mandatory payments to the associated secured creditor; 49 or
  • (4) the case involves a scheme to hinder, delay or defraud creditors with regard to real property. 50
Importantly, the Bankruptcy Rules do not require parties desiring relief from the automatic stay to institute an adversary proceeding as a condition to obtaining such relief. The Practitioner also should note a uniform consistency between the provisions for an automatic stay in section 362, the provisions for the use, sale, and lease of property contained in section 363, and the rules for adequate protection contained in section 361. Section 363(c)(1) provides that if the debtor in possession or trustee operates the debtor's business, the debtor or the trustee is allowed to utilize the property of the estate in the ordinary course of business. 53 Nonetheless, if the utilization of the debtor's assets jeopardizes other parties in interest, they may move the court to restrict or prohibit the use, sale, or lease of property by the debtor in possession or trustee unless adequate protection is furnished pursuant to section 361. 54 Likewise, a party in interest may move the court for relief from the stay for cause, including the lack of adequate protection as defined by section 361. 55 As they bear upon section 362's application, these provisions simply mean that the automatic stay is not set in stone, and it may be dissolved or modified at the request of creditors and parties in interest. 56
Lastly, it must be understood that, while stays of action outside the bankruptcy forum begin with an application of section 362, the power of the court to enjoin actions for the benefit of the estate does not end with that provision. As the legislative history itself suggests:
The court has ample other powers to stay actions not covered by the automatic stay. Section 105, of proposed title II, derived from Bankruptcy Act section 2a(15), grants the power to issue orders necessary or appropriate to carry out the provisions of title II. The bankruptcy courts are brought within the scope of the All Writs Statute, 28 U.S.C. 1651 (1970), and are given the powers of a court of law, equity, and admiralty. … Stays or injunctions issued under these sections will not be automatic upon the commencement of the case, but will be granted or issued under the usual rules for the issuances of injunctions. By excepting an act or action from the automatic stay, the bill simply requires the trustee to move the court into action, rather than requiring the stayed party to request relief from the stay. There are some actions, enumerated in the exceptions, that generally should not be stayed automatically upon the commencement of the case, for reasons of either policy or practicality. Thus, the court will have to determine on a case-by-case basis whether a particular action which may be harmful to the estate should be stayed. 57
These supplemental powers under Bankruptcy Code section 105 provide yet another means of reaching the automatic stay's twin goals: (1) to envelope the debtor's assets and operations within the protection of the bankruptcy court, and (2) to funnel all litigation concerning the debtor and the property of the estate into that same forum.
