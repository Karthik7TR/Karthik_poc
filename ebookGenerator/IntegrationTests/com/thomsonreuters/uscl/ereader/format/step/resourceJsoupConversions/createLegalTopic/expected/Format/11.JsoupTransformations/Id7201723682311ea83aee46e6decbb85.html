Chapter 2. Legal Bases Used by the Courts for the Protection of Confidential Business Information
IV. Contract Theory
§ 2:10. Express Contracts

The early cases were governed by the principle that information obtained in confidence or in the course of a confidential employment could not be made use of, either during the course of employment or at any subsequent time, to the detriment of the person from whom or at whose expense it was obtained. The concept was similar to the doctrine of “trust” and was found to be a property and or an equitable right. 1 In the 19th century, however, the equitable and property doctrines were overshadowed by contract cases, particularly in master and servant situations where the court implied an obligation of confidence which, in the master and servant cases, was usually related to the general duty of good faith. 2
In cases where the contract specifically provides that information be kept in confidence, modern British courts often favour the use of contract law as the rationale for the enforcement of a confidence or secret. In Printing and Numerical Registering Co. v. Sampson, 3 it was stated that:
… if there is one thing which more than another public policy requires it is that men of full age and competent understanding shall have the utmost liberty of contracting, and that their contracts when entered into freely and voluntarily shall be held sacred and shall be enforced by Courts of justice.
American courts have clearly recognized the right of persons to contract or pay for ideas. In Masline v. New York, New Haven & Hartford Railroad Co., 4 the plaintiff, an employee of the defendant's railroad, told the defendant that he had “information of value” and that, if used by the defendant, it would enable the defendant to earn at least $100,000 a year without any expense. The plaintiff agreed to provide the defendant with the information if the defendant would agree to give him 5% of the receipts gained from the use. The defendant agreed. The plaintiff advised the defendant that his idea was to sell advertising space in or on the railroad's stations, depots, rights of way, cars and fences. The defendant did so and began to earn a great deal of money. The defendant refused to pay any money to the plaintiff. At the trial, the court refused to enforce the agreement on the grounds that:
An idea may undoubtedly be protected by contract … but it must be the plaintiff's idea. Upon communication to the defendant it at once did appear that the idea was not original … but was a matter of common knowledge well known to the world at large. He had thought of nothing new and had therefore no property right to protect which would make his idea a basis of consideration for anything.
However, what the court seemed to be saying was not that an idea may not be the subject of a contract right, but rather that, in this case, the information was not confidential and thus would not be protected by the courts as confidential information belonging to the plaintiff.
A party may expressly agree to compensate another either for using an idea submitted by that party or for receiving an idea from the party without regard to its possible subsequent use. 5 The conveyance of an idea can also constitute valuable consideration and can be bargained for before it is disclosed to the proposed purchaser. Furthermore, where an idea has been conveyed with the expectation that compensation will be paid if the idea is used, there is no reason why the beneficiary of the conveyance of such an idea, who is profiting by it, may not, although he is not at the time under any legal obli-gation to do so, promise to pay a reasonable compensation for the past service and create a valid obligation. Some courts have held that when a party has expressly agreed to pay for the disclosure of an idea, the fact that the idea is not novel, original or concrete does not release the promisor from the obligation to pay. In other words, an idea may be the subject of a contractual right, whether or not there is a property right in the idea. 6 Other courts, however, like the court in Masline, 7 have held that “when one submits an idea to another, no promise to pay for its use may be implied, and no asserted agreement enforced if the elements of novelty and originality are absent, since the property right in an idea is based upon these two elements”. 8
