Preface
The Judicial Panel on Multidistrict Litigation is a unique, and important, part of the federal judiciary. The Panel exists to decide a single question: Where can cases pending in more than one district best be heard for pretrial proceedings? Its members include judges from around the country who already have one full-time job as district or circuit judge. The Panel handles thousands of cases and provides one of the most important means of efficient and fair case management in the federal judiciary. It regularly meets for one day of hearings a month and has, since its inception, handled over 2,700 separate dockets and tens of thousands of cases.
This work is in some ways the reincarnation of my earlier work on the Panel, Multidistrict Litigation, published in 1985 and long out of print. This new work covers the ascendent role of the Panel and many of its significant accomplishments during the past decades and provides detailed annotations to the Multidistrict Litigation statute, 28 U.S.C.A. § 1407. The Panel is publishing many of its decisions, and this work reviews those decisions and analyzes and explains the ins and outs of practice before the Panel. The Panel continues to play a central role in complex, multidistrict litigation, and recent legislation, including the Class Action Fairness Act of 2005, only enhances its ability to manage litigation.
This book is intended to provide guidance for the lawyer or party who faces the potential transfer of a case filed in the home district and now subject to transfer anywhere in the country. Because of the fundamentally practical nature of the Panel's mission, this book tries to answer as many of the practical questions litigants face. It is not a scholarly treatise—it is without apology a practice manual. In some cases, all this book may do is guide the reader to the Panel Rule that answers the question.
